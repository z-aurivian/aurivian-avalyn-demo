Intelligence Brief AP01 · AP02 · Inhaled Antifibrotics July 20, 2026 · 7:00 AM ET

Avalyn's inhaled-antifibrotic thesis is still unproven at Phase 3. United Therapeutics just proved inhaled delivery works for pulmonary fibrosis — for a different drug entirely. Does that de-risk Avalyn's story, or raise the bar it now has to clear alone?

MIST (AP01, Phase 2b) completed enrollment in June with topline FVC data not expected until the second half of 2027. AURA-IPF (AP02) dosed its first patient in March. In the meantime, United Therapeutics' TETON-1 and TETON-2 — inhaled treprostinil, a prostacyclin, not an antifibrotic — both met their primary FVC endpoint in IPF. Pulmonologists now have a live example that inhaled delivery can move the needle in progressive fibrosis. Whether that reads as validating precedent or as a bar Avalyn hasn't cleared yet is being decided in KOL conversations right now, well before MIST has anything to show.

The central tension: TETON's Phase 3 win is the first proof that a Phase 3, placebo-controlled trial of an inhaled therapy can move FVC in progressive pulmonary fibrosis — but it is a prostacyclin, not an antifibrotic, and the mechanism gap is exactly the kind of nuance that gets lost the further a narrative travels from an academic ILD center. AP01/AP02 still have to prove their own thesis at Phase 2b before any of that borrowed credibility converts into something real.

Providers
↑ Critical Window · TETON Readout

Cautious optimism on ATLAS's dose-response signal is colliding with a credibility question TETON just sharpened.

ATLAS, AP01's earlier Phase 1b trial (published in Thorax, 2023), showed near-stabilized FVC through 48 weeks at the 100mg BID dose versus continued decline at the lower dose — a real dose-response signal, though uncontrolled and without a placebo arm. MIST (Phase 2b, 375 patients) has now completed enrollment with FVC-at-week-52 topline expected in the second half of 2027; AURA-IPF (AP02, Phase 2) dosed its first patient in March. Against that still-early evidence base, TETON-1 and TETON-2 — United Therapeutics' inhaled treprostinil program — both met their primary FVC endpoint in IPF in a Phase 3, placebo-controlled design, published in NEJM.

What this means: TETON proves the lung is a credible target for a PF-modifying inhaled therapy at Phase 3 scale — but for a prostacyclin acting on pulmonary vasculature, not an antifibrotic acting on fibrotic tissue remodeling. The KOL most likely to either draw the wrong equivalence or sharpen the right distinction is Dr. Steven Nathan (Inova Fairfax), TETON's principal investigator, who per Aurivian's own KOL tracking currently reads as skeptical and trending away on AP02 specifically — the ILD voice with the most first-hand reason to know exactly how different these two mechanisms are. Meanwhile Dr. Ganesh Raghu (guideline chair), Dr. Toby Maher, and Dr. Luca Richeldi are treating MIST, not TETON, as the actual test of Avalyn's thesis.

"TETON tells us patients will tolerate a nebulized therapy for a fibrosing lung disease long enough for it to matter. It doesn't tell us pirfenidone survives that trip through the airway the same way a prostacyclin does. Those are two different pharmacology questions wearing the same delivery device."

ILD specialist · academic medical center, US · June 2026

Patients
● New Narrative

Adherence fatigue with oral antifibrotics is real. Patient advocates have heard "better tolerated" before, and it hasn't always held up.

Oral antifibrotic tolerability burden is well documented: pirfenidone discontinuation runs 14.6% versus 9.6% on placebo, driven substantially by nausea (36%) and photosensitive rash (30%); nintedanib carries a 62–76% diarrhea rate and a 21% discontinuation rate versus 15% on placebo. IPF and SSc-ILD patient advocacy communities are increasingly vocal about this day-to-day burden as a quality-of-life issue distinct from the disease itself, not just a side note to it.

What's at stake: the inhaled-route pitch is that lower systemic exposure (per Avalyn's own company-reported PK figures) should translate into a materially lighter side-effect burden. But this patient community has recent memory of other "better tolerated" candidates that did not pan out — FibroGen's pamrevlumab failed Phase 3 in 2023, and Pliant's bexotegrast was discontinued in June 2025 after a safety signal. Any tolerability message aimed at patients needs to be earned with data at readout, not asserted ahead of it.

"We've been told 'this one will be easier on your body' before. I'm not against hope — I'm against hope with no data behind it yet. Show us the Phase 2 numbers and I'll believe it."

IPF patient advocate · patient support community moderator, US · May 2026
→ Field Validation Question

Payers
→ Stable / Early

No product, no price yet — but the device-reimbursement precedent is already being written by Tyvaso DPI. Avalyn's future pricing conversation has an anchor before it has a product.

No Avalyn asset has an FDA approval or a pricing conversation to speak of — MIST's earliest topline is 18 months out. But inhaled specialty respiratory drugs administered by device already have a reimbursement pattern payers apply by analogy: device/administration billing codes, in-clinic versus at-home administration, and training burden, all shaped in practice by how Tyvaso DPI is currently reimbursed. That precedent will color how AP01/AP02 get modeled long before either has a label.

What's at stake: if MIST reads out positive, Avalyn's launch-readiness conversation with payers won't start from a blank slate — it starts from an analogy question. Will AP01/AP02 be billed like a specialty inhaled device-administered product, or will nerandomilast (Boehringer's newly approved oral, new-mechanism antifibrotic) reset the "novel mechanism premium" conversation in a way that pulls pricing logic back toward oral comparators instead?

"Every new inhaled respiratory therapy gets measured against Tyvaso DPI's administration cost model right now, whether or not the comparison is clinically fair. That's just where the reimbursement conversation starts."

Payer/health-economics analyst · specialty pharmacy industry publication · April 2026

When a P&T-committee-adjacent contact models early inhaled-PF-therapy economics, are they anchoring to Tyvaso DPI's device/administration cost basis, or to oral antifibrotic WAC comparisons — because that anchor point will shape how AP01/AP02 get priced years before launch.

Competitive
↑↑ Accelerating Signal

TETON's win, nerandomilast's approval, and Esbriet's generic slide are moving three ends of the board at once. None of them is a like-for-like competitor — and that's exactly the problem.

TETON-1/TETON-2 (United Therapeutics, inhaled treprostinil) met their primary FVC endpoint in IPF. Insmed's TPIP (inhaled treprostinil palmitil) is advancing through Phase 2 in PPF/IPF and Phase 3 in PH-ILD — an earlier-stage but directly analogous inhaled-delivery competitor. Boehringer Ingelheim's nerandomilast (Jascayd), a PDE4B inhibitor, was approved for IPF in October 2025 and PPF in December 2025 — the first genuinely new IPF mechanism in a decade. Roche's Esbriet (oral pirfenidone) is meanwhile losing share to generic erosion, and Bristol Myers Squibb's admilparant, an oral LPA1 antagonist, is in Phase 3 for both IPF and PPF.

What this means: Avalyn's "no like-for-like inhaled-antifibrotic competitor" position is real today — but it is temporary comfort, because it also means no one has proven the inhaled-antifibrotic thesis, for better or worse. The two developments most consequential to AP01/AP02 over the next 18 months are not Avalyn's own: whether MIST's own topline (H2 2027) reads out positive, and how thoroughly nerandomilast displaces Ofev/Esbriet as the oral standard that any inhaled option eventually gets compared against.

"Everyone's watching TETON because it's the flashy delivery story. But nerandomilast is the one quietly resetting what 'good enough efficacy' even means in this category — and it did it with a pill, not a nebulizer."

Competitive intelligence analyst · industry conference sidebar conversation · June 2026

Intelligence Brief Competitive Signal United Therapeutics · TETON-1 / TETON-2 July 20, 2026 · 7:00 AM ET

TETON-1 and TETON-2 met their primary FVC endpoint. Inhaled delivery for progressive pulmonary fibrosis is no longer hypothetical — it's precedent, for a drug that isn't Avalyn's.

United Therapeutics' inhaled treprostinil program — already marketed for pulmonary arterial hypertension as Tyvaso/Tyvaso DPI — extended into IPF with TETON-1 and TETON-2, and both met their primary FVC endpoint, published in NEJM. It is the most advanced inhaled therapy to demonstrate a Phase 3 effect in a fibrosing lung disease. It is also a prostacyclin, not an antifibrotic, and its mechanism has nothing to do with the fibrotic remodeling pathway AP01 and AP02 are built to address. The result matters to Avalyn primarily as a narrative event, not a clinical one.

The strategic picture: TETON is the single most useful reference point ILD KOLs now have for "does inhaled delivery move outcomes in fibrosing lung disease," and it will be cited — correctly or not — every time AP01/AP02 come up in conversation. Dr. Steven Nathan, TETON's principal investigator, is tracked in Avalyn's own KOL data as skeptical and trending away on AP02 specifically, which makes him simultaneously the highest-risk and highest-value conversation Avalyn's field team can have this quarter.

The mechanism gap is the whole story. Prostacyclin vasodilation and antifibrotic tissue remodeling are different biology wearing the same nebulizer.

Treprostinil is a prostacyclin analog acting on pulmonary vasculature — its established mechanism in PAH. Its extension into IPF via TETON tests whether that vasodilatory/anti-remodeling activity slows fibrosis progression by a different biological route than pirfenidone or nintedanib. A positive FVC result in TETON says the lung can be a productive delivery target for a Phase 3 inhaled therapy in this disease family. It says nothing, mechanistically, about whether reformulating an already-approved oral antifibrotic for inhaled delivery will preserve that drug's own efficacy while cutting systemic exposure — which is the actual bet MIST and AURA-IPF are testing.
